Definition

What is the Euclidean algorithm?

The Euclidean algorithm is a method for finding the greatest common factor of two integers by repeatedly dividing and replacing the pair with the divisor and the remainder.

If a = bq + r, then gcd(a, b) = gcd(b, r). When the remainder becomes zero, the current divisor is the GCF.

Why it works

Common divisors survive each step.

Any number that divides both original integers must also divide their remainder, so the set of common divisors stays the same from one step to the next.

That makes the algorithm both correct and efficient, even for large inputs.

Ancient Roots

Documented in Euclid's Elements around 300 BCE.

Its original framing was geometric, long before algebraic notation became standard.

Algorithmic Power

One of history’s earliest formal algorithms.

It became a model for computational thinking: clear steps, guaranteed progress, dependable output.

Modern Relevance

Still used in coding, cryptography, and theorem proving.

Its logic appears in modular arithmetic, RSA, and efficient simplification of rational numbers.

Animated timeline

A fast history of the Euclidean algorithm.

c. 300 BCE

Euclid writes Elements

The algorithm appears in Book VII, where number relationships are explored with extraordinary rigor.

Middle Ages

Transmission across cultures

Greek mathematical ideas, including Euclid’s work, were studied, translated, and preserved across the Mediterranean world.

19th-20th Century

Formal algorithmic thinking

Mathematicians and computer scientists highlighted the Euclidean algorithm as a classic example of efficient stepwise computation.

Today

Core tool in computing

It remains foundational in coding interviews, symbolic algebra systems, number theory, and cryptographic protocols.
